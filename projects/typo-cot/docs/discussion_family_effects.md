## Model Family Effects

Our experiments surface three findings that jointly suggest the internal pipeline by which LLMs process typographical perturbations in Chain-of-Thought reasoning may be architecture-dependent: repair speed, attribution structure, and the pathway from intermediate reasoning to answer derivation appear to differ across model families. We discuss each finding in turn, then synthesize their joint implications.

**Scope of the family-level inference (read as contrast cases, not a correlation).** These family-level observations rest on only **five models spanning three families** (Llama, Gemma, Mistral). We therefore present the double dissociations below as **contrast cases**, not as a correlational or regression result: fitting a trend across five model-points and reading off a family effect would be an ecological fallacy at this $n$. Accordingly we avoid categorical ("proves", "establishes") phrasing and use hedged language throughout this subsection. The claims are promoted from contrast cases to sample- and setting-level evidence only once Experiments 12 (R_C composition) and 13 (read-out concentration) provide within-family, per-setting distributions; until then the family differences are hypotheses motivated by convergent contrast cases, not confirmed effects.

**Mistral's fixed-target reversal.**
Experiment 4 examined how the correlation between CoT token overlap (Jaccard@10) and answer flip changes when the attribution target is fixed to the original clean answer rather than allowed to co-vary with the generated answer. Under default (unfixed) attribution, the target answer shifts whenever the model flips its response, introducing a potential selection bias: tokens that appear relevant to the answer may do so partly because the answer itself has changed, altering which tokens fall into the top-$k$ overlap set. Fixing the target to the clean answer removes this confound. For Gemma and Llama, removing the bias attenuates the correlation (Gemma MC $\Delta\rho = +0.371$; Llama MC $\Delta\rho = +0.575$), indicating that a portion of their original signal was artifactual. For Mistral, however, the correlation *strengthens* under the fixed target (pooled $\Delta\rho = -0.077$, 95\% CI $[-0.127, -0.028]$, $t = -2.90$, $p = 0.044$). A two-way ANOVA confirms a significant family $\times$ format interaction ($\eta^2 = 0.066$, $p = 8.9 \times 10^{-5}$) alongside a large family main effect ($\eta^2 = 0.397$, $p = 1.2 \times 10^{-10}$). This reversal suggests that for Mistral, the default measurement was not merely noisy but actively *masking* the true relationship between CoT perturbation and answer determination. A likely explanation connects to Mistral's anomalous logit lens behavior: its unembedding matrix yields clean-self minimum ranks of 0.27--0.50, far below the 0.86--0.97 range observed for Llama and Gemma. In other words, Mistral's residual stream representations do not straightforwardly decode into the tokens that the model ultimately produces [REF:nostalgebraist2020], making surface-level token overlap an unreliable proxy for internal causal structure unless the attribution target is carefully controlled. This finding underscores that evaluation metrics for CoT faithfulness [REF:Lanham2023] may yield systematically different conclusions depending on the model family under study.

**Spatial separation of perturbation propagation and answer determination.**
Experiment 3 investigated where in the CoT sequence perturbation effects manifest (measured by KL divergence between clean and perturbed token distributions) versus where answer-critical reasoning occurs (measured by $R_C$ token importance). Across all 60 experimental settings, these two distributions are statistically significantly separated ($p < 10^{-10}$). Globally, KL divergence peaks in the front half of the CoT (mean normalized position 0.359), while $R_C$ peaks in the back half (mean position 0.541), yielding a Wasserstein distance of 0.182. The separation is most pronounced for GSM8K under importance-targeted perturbation (LXT-4), where $R_C$ concentrates at positions 0.73--0.78 near the final answer, while KL peaks at 0.26--0.34, producing a Wasserstein distance of up to 0.47. Under importance perturbation, KL front fractions range from 65\% to 87\% across models, confirming that perturbation effects propagate early---during problem restatement and initial reasoning steps---while answer-determining tokens cluster in the final reasoning stages. This spatial separation transforms what initially appeared to be a negative result (low precision@10 for predicting answer flips from KL peaks) into a structural insight: the CoT sequence comprises functionally distinct stages, and the locus of perturbation propagation is systematically upstream of the locus of causal answer determination. Notably, model families differ in the degree of back-loading: Gemma and Llama show clear $R_C$ concentration in the latter half (front fraction 0.11--0.46), while Mistral distributes $R_C$ more uniformly (front fraction $\approx$ 0.50), consistent with its atypical internal representation structure discussed above.

**Gemma's ultra-fast but unstable repair.**
Experiment 9 traced how perturbed word representations are repaired across layers by measuring cosine similarity to the corresponding clean representations. Gemma exhibits strikingly rapid repair: it reaches the $\cos > 0.95$ threshold by layer 5.3 out of approximately 31 layers (17\% of network depth), with 100\% of perturbed words achieving this ceiling. By contrast, only 6.9\% of Llama's perturbed words and 17.9\% of Mistral's reach the same threshold. This ceiling effect causes Gemma's repair\_score (maximum cosine similarity) to saturate at 0.99, eliminating its discriminative power between answer-flip and no-flip cases. For Llama and Mistral, repair\_score functions as a strong predictor of answer stability (pooled coefficient $= -0.977$, $p = 3.7 \times 10^{-86}$). Crucially, however, Gemma's rapid repair is not synonymous with robust repair. Gemma exhibits a pronounced final-layer cosine drop of 0.272 on average (flip: 0.291, no-flip: 0.267, $p = 10^{-41}$), compared to drops of merely 0.017 for Llama and 0.038 for Mistral. This suggests that Gemma's layer normalization architecture [REF:Tsuji2025] creates a compressed representation space in which cosine similarity reaches ceiling rapidly in intermediate layers, but the representations subsequently degrade at the output projection. The discriminative signal for Gemma resides not in repair presence (which is universal) but in repair *quality*: the middle-layer cosine segment yields a Cohen's $d = -0.475$, the strongest single discriminant, indicating that the gradient of repair through the network's middle layers---rather than its asymptotic maximum---captures meaningful variation in processing fidelity.

**Synthesis.**
Taken together, and read as the contrast cases they are (five models, three families; see the scope note above), these three findings are consistent with distinct typo-processing profiles for each model family. In the observed cases, Llama and Gemma exhibit a "canonical" pattern in which CoT token overlap with the answer is partly mediated by target co-variation, perturbation effects and answer determination are spatially segregated, and repair dynamics (whether slow-and-discriminative as in Llama or fast-and-saturating as in Gemma) follow architecturally predictable trajectories. Mistral departs from this pattern at every level of analysis: its token overlap signal is masked rather than inflated by default attribution, its $R_C$ distribution lacks the back-loading seen in other families, and its repair dynamics occupy an intermediate regime. We hypothesize that these differences trace to Mistral's distinctive internal representation geometry, as evidenced by its anomalous logit lens ranks, which may reflect differences in how the sliding window attention mechanism and the unembedding matrix interact with perturbed inputs [REF:Jiang2023]. Gemma's profile, meanwhile, suggests a trade-off between repair speed and repair stability: its architecture achieves rapid convergence toward clean representations but at the cost of output-layer fidelity, a pattern consistent with the "inner lexicon" hypothesis [REF:Kaplan2025] in which early-layer repair operates on a shared representational substrate that diverges from the task-specific representations needed at the output layer.

These family-level differences carry practical implications for future work on typo robustness. First, they suggest that a single evaluation metric for CoT faithfulness under perturbation is unlikely to generalize across architectures; researchers should report family-stratified results and consider architecture-specific attribution targets. Second, the spatial separation of perturbation propagation and answer determination implies that interventions aimed at improving typo robustness---such as representation denoising [REF:Tsuji2025] or input normalization---should target the early CoT stages where perturbation effects concentrate, rather than the later stages where answers are determined. Third, the Gemma repair profile cautions against interpreting high intermediate-layer similarity as evidence of successful error correction; output-layer fidelity must be independently verified, particularly for architectures employing aggressive normalization. More broadly, our results motivate the development of architecture-aware benchmarks for reasoning robustness, in which evaluation protocols are calibrated to the internal processing characteristics of each model family rather than applied uniformly.
